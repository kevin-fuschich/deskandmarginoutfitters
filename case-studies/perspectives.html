Case Study

Perspectives With the President

Turning private conversations into shared truth inside a performance-driven culture.

Context

National General operated as a performance-driven, execution-first organization. Employees described the culture as heads-down and direct, with little tolerance for unnecessary process.

As the company scaled, communication increased—but connection did not. Employees were informed, but not convinced leadership understood their reality.

The Problem

Town halls existed, but questions were filtered, safe, and predictable. Real concerns lived elsewhere—in smaller conversations that never scaled.

The issue was not communication volume. It was lack of access.

The Intervention

Small-group conversations (4–5 employees, mixed levels and functions) with no script, no pre-screened questions, and no requirement to ask a question at all.

Participants could simply state what they were experiencing.

The Breakthrough

The conversations were recorded and shared internally as audio.

Audio removed performance, distraction, and friction. Employees could listen on their own time, without visual noise or pressure.

Listen

A moment from one of the conversations.

Unscripted. Unfiltered. Exactly as shared internally.

Distribution

Each session was lightly produced with an intro and close, positioned as an internal podcast.

Distribution included President communication, intranet placement, and leadership sharing.

The most effective channel: managers referencing conversations in team settings.

The conversations were not just seen—they were carried.

System

Small Room → Real Question → Direct Response → Recorded Conversation → Light Production → Multi-Channel Distribution → Manager Amplification → Shared Understanding

Real Moments

“Why does it feel like decisions are made without understanding how our systems actually work?”
“We’re being asked to move faster, but we don’t always have clarity on what matters most.”

Outcome

Employees began referencing shared conversations. Leaders gained earlier visibility into real concerns. Questions became more direct across the organization.

The Lesson

Most organizations try to distribute information.

The more effective approach is to create something worth carrying—and let managers carry it.
